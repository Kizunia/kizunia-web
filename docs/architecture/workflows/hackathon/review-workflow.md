# Hackathon Review Workflow

## Purpose

This document describes how community-submitted hackathon suggestions are reviewed before becoming publicly available on Kizunia Web.

The review workflow exists to maintain the quality, accuracy, and trustworthiness of published hackathon information while keeping community contributions simple and accessible.

Every published hackathon should pass through this workflow unless explicitly created by a platform administrator.

---

# Goals

The review workflow aims to:

- Ensure published information is accurate.
- Prevent spam and malicious submissions.
- Reduce duplicate hackathons.
- Allow contributors to improve their submissions.
- Minimize administrative effort.
- Maintain transparency throughout the review process.

---

# Actors

## Community User

The contributor who submitted the hackathon.

The contributor may:

- View submission status.
- Respond to requested changes.
- Resubmit updated information.

The contributor cannot approve or publish their own submission.

---

## Administrator

Administrators are responsible for reviewing community submissions.

They may:

- Review submissions.
- Edit submitted information before publication.
- Approve submissions.
- Reject submissions.
- Request changes.

---

## Platform Owner

The Platform Owner has unrestricted access to the review system and may perform every administrator action.

---

# Review Queue

Every submitted suggestion enters the review queue.

The queue acts as the central moderation area for hackathon suggestions.

Each item in the queue should display enough information for an administrator to quickly understand the submission without opening it.

Example information:

- Title
- Organizer
- Submitted By
- Submission Time
- Current Status
- Completion Level
- Duplicate Detection Result

The review queue should prioritize clarity rather than density.

---

# Opening a Submission

When an administrator opens a submission, they should be presented with two views.

## Submitted Information

The original information provided by the contributor.

This preserves the contributor's submission exactly as it was submitted.

---

## Preview

A live preview of how the hackathon page will appear after publication.

Administrators should review the content in its final presentation rather than reading raw database fields.

---

# Administrator Actions

Administrators have four possible actions.

---

## Approve

Approving a submission creates a new Hackathon using the reviewed information.

The proposal is marked as approved.

The contributor is notified.

---

## Request Changes

If additional information or corrections are required, the administrator may request changes.

Examples include:

- Incorrect dates.
- Missing registration link.
- Broken website.
- Low quality description.
- Incorrect location.

The contributor receives feedback and may update the submission before submitting it again.

---

## Reject

Administrators may reject a submission.

Examples include:

- Spam.
- Duplicate submission.
- Fake event.
- Insufficient information.
- Event no longer exists.

Rejected submissions remain visible to their creator together with the rejection reason.

---

## Save Progress

Administrators may save their review without making a final decision.

This allows reviews to continue later without affecting the contributor.

---

# Editing During Review

Administrators may improve submitted information before publication.

Examples include:

- Formatting descriptions.
- Correcting spelling.
- Improving categorization.
- Uploading a better banner.
- Adding official links.

Administrative edits should improve the submission without changing its intent.

---

# Notifications

Contributors should receive notifications whenever the review status changes.

Examples include:

- Submission received.
- Changes requested.
- Submission approved.
- Submission rejected.

Notifications improve transparency and encourage future contributions.

---

# Review Principles

Administrators should follow several principles during review.

## Assume Good Intent

Most contributors are attempting to help the community.

Minor mistakes should be corrected rather than rejected whenever possible.

---

## Prefer Request Changes

Whenever practical, request additional information instead of rejecting the submission outright.

This creates a better contributor experience.

---

## Preserve Accuracy

Published information should always prioritize accuracy over speed.

When uncertainty exists, administrators should verify information using official sources.

---

## Be Transparent

Whenever a submission is rejected or requires changes, administrators should explain why.

Constructive feedback encourages future participation.

---

# Edge Cases

Examples include:

- Multiple submissions for the same hackathon.
- Organizer submits after community member.
- Registration closes during review.
- Official website changes.
- Event cancelled before publication.

These situations should be handled while preserving the integrity of published information.

---

# Out of Scope

This document does not cover:

- Duplicate detection algorithms.
- Hackathon lifecycle.
- Organizer edit permissions.
- Community edit suggestions.
- Database schema.

These topics are documented separately.

---

# Guiding Principle

The review workflow should make publishing trustworthy without making contributing difficult.

Community members should feel encouraged to contribute, while administrators should have the tools necessary to efficiently curate accurate and reliable hackathon information.
